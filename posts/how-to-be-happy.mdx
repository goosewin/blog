## Prelude: Why This Essay Exists

Six months ago I boxed my life, left the country-side life behind, and landed in San Francisco with a few suitcases, and zero friends within a hundred-mile radius. SF is a strange magnet: the place hums with people who believe—often to the edge of delusion—that their side-project can dent the universe. That raw conviction made me ask a harder question than _How do I win?_ It made me ask _How do I stay fulfilled while I'm winning?_

Around the same time I started working closely with a mentor at Deaglo who lives by what he calls the **4 L's Principle**—**Live, Love, Learn, Legacy**. The idea is simple: if even one “L” is chronically empty, the rest eventually drain. I realized I was over-indexed on _Learn_ (endless up-skilling) and _Live_ (24/7 shipping), but way under on _Love_ and _Legacy_. Cue a season of soul-searching: I moved cities, rebooted my social circle, picked up weight-lifting, and launched **NextDev.fm** to connect builders worldwide.

This essay is the running journal of that experiment. It borrows the numbered-rule format of Sam Altman's legendary “How to Be Successful,” but the target metric here isn't unicorn valuations—it's waking up stoked more days than not. ([Sam Altman][1])

---

## 1. Find Your Obsession

Every happy high-achiever I know is a little bit possessed. Obsession is the flywheel: it pulls you out of bed before the alarm and keeps you coding long past the “reasonable” stopping point. Think of it as personal compounding—energy reinvested creates more energy tomorrow. ([Sam Altman][1])

If nothing grabs you yet, run fast experiments: build a weekend SaaS, volunteer at a food pantry, shadow a photographer. Treat interest like A/B tests—ship, measure dopamine, iterate. One warning: the perfect obsession will _not_ ask your permission. The first time I wrote a goofy programming meme and 10,000 strangers liked it, I knew I'd found a rabbit hole worth spelunking.

Obsession also immunizes you against external scoreboards. When you're genuinely fixated, you stop counting Twitter likes and start counting learning curves. That single shift moves happiness from fragile (someone else's approval) to anti-fragile (your own curiosity).

---

## 2. Commit to Deep Focus

Attention is the single non-renewable resource we squander most quickly. Once you identify meaningful work, allocate the lion's share of your waking hours to it and engineer your environment like a paranoid security expert: kill notifications, schedule Wi-Fi-off sprints, batch meetings into one afternoon a week. Focus, as Altman notes, is a force multiplier—doing the right work beats doing _more_ work. ([Sam Altman][1])

There's another layer: emotional focus. Decide—publicly if helpful—that this craft is your top priority for the next season. Social pressure forces consistency. I once tweeted that I'd ship an open-source recruiter agent by Q3. The fear of public failure shaved hours of procrastination off my calendar.

Remember: distractions don't just steal minutes; they fracture narrative. Fragmented stories rarely feel satisfying. A contiguous block of deep labor, on the other hand, delivers the flow state psychologists describe as the closest everyday humans get to bliss.

---

## 3. Ship Early, Ship Often, Feel It Land

Progress without feedback is just theory. The faster you expose your work to reality—users, audiences, barbells—the quicker fulfillment loops back. My first 45-pound incline press changed nothing in the world, yet the immediate kinesthetic “Whoa, I can do that!” rewired my self-image. Posting that same lift on social media sparked DMs from strangers starting their own routines. Output begets impact; impact begets meaning.

Concrete targets help:

- **Code** — Merge a pull request every Friday, no matter how small, and watch your confidence snowball.
- **Content** — Publish one meme or blog post weekly; even mild virality feels like a live-wire jolt.
- **Fitness** — Add five pounds to the bar each cycle; micro-victories aggregate into visible transformation.

Finished things also create optionality. An MVP can pivot; an un-shipped prototype just creates guilt. Shipping is both emotional catharsis _and_ strategic leverage.

---

## 4. Master Your Money

Yes, cash influences happiness—mostly by deleting anxiety. What kills joy isn't _not_ having a yacht; it's overdrafting two days before rent. The fix: ruthless financial literacy. Track every transaction for 30 days. Automate transfers to an “Oh-Shit Fund” that covers three months of expenses.

Why the frugality sermon? Because obsession, focus, and shipping all thrive when you _can_ choose curiosity over quick cash. Altman calls this **making it easy to take risks**—keep personal burn low so you can chase 100-to-1 upside bets. ([Sam Altman][1])

Once your baseline is secure, remember money's diminishing returns. A second-hand standing desk might boost health and mood far more than five extra delivery meals. Spend on leverage, not vanity.

---

## 5. Spread Positivity—“As You Climb, Lift”

Negativity has a half-life; positivity is nuclear fission. Smile after a sleepless sprint, review a junior dev's PR with genuine care, send a handwritten thank-you when someone open-sources a library you love. Tiny deposits compound into reputational capital, and—surprise—making someone else's day improves yours via the helper's-high effect.

My mentor's refrain, “As you climb, lift,” is now company canon at Deaglo. Last month our intern shipped a tiny UI polish that shaved three clicks off an investor workflow. We made a Slack party out of it, and her increased velocity since has paid back the fifteen-minute celebration tenfold.

Positivity isn't fakery; it's selective highlight. The world will happily advertise your problems. You get to amplify the bright spots.

---

## 6. Move the Meat Suit

Humans evolved to chase antelope, not scroll dashboards. Paradoxically, the less physical labor modern life demands, the more deliberate we must be about movement. Thirty minutes of strength or cardio four times a week boosts mood-regulating neurotransmitters, improves sleep, and reinforces self-efficacy—the belief that effort changes outcomes.

I thought weight-lifting would siphon time from coding; instead it injected stamina _into_ coding. After heavy squats, context-switch anxiety drops, and I write cleaner APIs. Treat exercise like brushing teeth: non-negotiable maintenance of hardware that runs your mental software.

---

## 7. Leave the Algorithm, Meet Humans

Serendipity lives in analog spaces. Every pivotal career break I've had began with showing up somewhere I could've rationalized skipping: a last-minute hackathon, a community meetup, a random dinner after a conference. New environments jolt pattern recognition, prime creativity, and seed collaborations impossible inside social-media homophily.

Action item: attend one in-person event this month where you know exactly zero attendees. Bring curiosity, not business cards. The ROI might be a friend, a mentor, or an idea that reroutes your roadmap. Worst case you have an awkward story—great podcast fodder.

---

## 8. Teach to Learn—Double-Entry Knowledge

When you explain a concept, you discover the edges of your understanding. Pair-program with a junior dev, start a 30-day thread summarizing ML papers, coach a gym newbie on deadlift form. The universe rewards open-source mindsets: mentors appear, feedback refines your craft, and opportunities surface from people who like learning _with_ you.

Teaching also solves the loneliness problem. Collaborative growth stitches _Love_ and _Legacy_ together: you bond over shared struggle, and your knowledge outlives the session.

---

## 9. Iterate Your Identity

There's a persistent myth that personality is static after college. The neuroscience says otherwise: adult brains remain plastic, and happiness correlates with environments that encourage growth. Think of yourself as a product in perpetual beta. Quarterly retros: What energized me? What drained me? Ship version N+1 accordingly.

Concrete tactics:

- **30-Day Trials** — Commit to a hobby long enough to push past beginner friction, then renew or retire.
- **Geographic Shuffles** — A new city scrambles defaults; even a month-long sublet can reboot habits.
- **Narrative Edits** — If your self-story casts you as “the backend guy,” try a design sprint. Surprise yourself.

Change is not betrayal of past selves; it's the software update that fixes yesterday's bugs.

---

## 10. Build a Legacy—The Fourth L

Legacy is emotional compound interest: the value you deposit into other people's lives keeps paying out after you log off. This doesn't mandate running a philanthropy. It can be smaller: mentoring the intern who becomes a staff engineer, publishing a library that saves strangers hours, documenting family history before stories fade.

Remember Altman's note on being _internally driven_—aim for impact that would satisfy you even if no one tweeted about it. ([Sam Altman][1]) Legacy satisfies because it aligns effort with meaning, not applause.

Ask yourself quarterly: “If I vanished tomorrow, what continues because I existed?” If the answer feels thin, allocate cycles to enrich it.

---

## Final Word: Live, Love, Learn, Leave Something Better

Happiness is not a glitchy lottery win; it's an iterative build that loops through the 4 L's. **Live fully** by obsessing and focusing. **Love generously** by lifting others. **Learn relentlessly** through teaching and physical challenge. Then channel the surplus into **Legacy**, so future humans start one notch higher.

I'm only 25—barely out of tutorial mode—but this framework already steers my daily decisions: ship the meme, take the intro call, add ten pounds to the bar, write the doc so the next dev moves faster.
